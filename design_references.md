# Design References — WHATS Card Tool

*Read this file at the start of any design-focused session. It documents two visual references and how they inform the next design pass on the WHATS tool.*

---

## Current Aesthetic Baseline

The tool currently uses a Mac System 6 / Risograph / 80s sports-comic language: hard black borders, offset drop shadows, halftone dot backgrounds, and a limited colour palette. The references below suggest how to deepen and mature that language without abandoning it.

---

## Reference 1 — GT Mechanik (Grilli Type)

**Source:** https://gt-mechanik.com/ (dedicated minisite) + https://www.grillitype.com/typeface/gt-mechanik
**What it is:** A variable type family released in 2026 by Grilli Type (offices in Brooklyn, NY and Lucerne, CH), designed by Shiva Nallaperumal, Reto Moser, and Noël Leu. Three subfamilies — Mono, Semi, Poly — share a common mechanical DNA expressed across a custom TONE axis (values 1–100), a weight axis (Thin to Black, i.e. 100–900), and a Slant axis (0–6). The family comprises 42 styles in total, with 7 weights and obliques per subfamily.

### The Minisite as Design Object

The dedicated site at gt-mechanik.com functions as an editorial specimen — not a conventional product page but a staged transmission. The structure runs in a single vertical scroll through distinct named sections: a type tester, subfamily demonstrations (Mono, Semi, Poly), Inktraps, Contrast, and Family. The overall impression is of a communications system coming online: the page announces itself rather than presenting itself. Every design decision reinforces the mechanical-broadcast fiction.

### Colour Palette and Visual Tone

The site operates in a high-contrast monochrome register — almost certainly black text on white or off-white ground, or the inverse, consistent with Grilli Type's wider house aesthetic of maximum contrast and no decorative colour. No accent colours or tints are introduced. The only visual relief comes from the typeface itself at varying weights and TONE settings. This is a deliberately one-colour approach: the "colour" is typographic density. If there is any variation, it is between pure black and structured white space.

The numerical labels used for navigation (87.8, 91.2, 95.6, 97.7, 99.2 assigned to Mono, Semi, Poly, and Info sections) function as a pseudo-scientific reference scale, treating the page's own sections as if they were calibrated instrument readings. These are displayed as a horizontal navigation strip at or near the top, set in the typeface itself.

### Typography: Specimen and Display Strategy

The site uses GT Mechanik throughout — the typeface is both subject and medium. Key typographic observations:

- **All-caps section labels:** MONO, SEMI, POLY, TONE, UNIT ID, SERIAL, INCOMING BROADCAST FROM — all structural labels appear in full uppercase, reinforcing a machine-readout register.
- **Size hierarchy:** Section headings appear at large display sizes; body copy at a modest reading size. The contrast between them is extreme — no intermediate text size softens the step.
- **Letter-spacing:** Uppercase labels appear with open tracking, consistent with the mechanical/teleprinter aesthetic where characters are individually stamped.
- **The TONE axis at 1, 33, 100:** The Semi section uses these three calibration points to demonstrate the spectrum. At TONE 1, letterforms are at their most mechanical and monospaced; at TONE 100, they approach the geometric, rhythmic quality of Poly. This is shown directly in specimen text, not just described.
- **Oversized punctuation as structural accent:** The question mark in "Hello. Is anybody there?" and the full stops and colons throughout are noticeably large relative to the cap height — this is a designed-in characteristic of Mono especially. The punctuation reads as visual emphasis, not merely syntactic.
- **No decorative typefaces:** The entire UI — navigation, labels, body copy, specimen text — is set in GT Mechanik. There is no secondary typeface. The typeface demonstrates itself.

### Specimen Copy: Broadcast Fiction

The most distinctive element of the site is the editorial voice of its copy. Rather than conventional specimen lorem-ipsum or generic marketing language, the site constructs a fictional communications scenario:

> "Hello. Is anybody there?"

> "The channel is open, stable, and receiving. Signal detected. If this message reaches you, know that the connection is working exactly as intended. We are broadcasting into a shared field, where every presence adds structure, every response adds meaning."

Other specimen phrases used across the page: **"Medium is the message"** (in the Poly section), **"calibrating calibrating calibrating"** (in the Semi section). A unit reference reads **"UNIT ID: RC-P73/NYC — SERIAL: 004981-AX"**, styled as a technical identifier. Time stamps appear: **"18:52 60:00"**. A prompt: **"Confirm? Yes"**.

This vocabulary — broadcast, signal, calibration, channel, tone, transmission — maps directly onto the typeface's own technical concepts (the TONE axis, the Mono/Semi/Poly spectrum) while creating an immersive fiction that you are reading a machine's attempt to make contact. The effect is dry, deadpan, and quietly funny.

### Subfamily Descriptions: Design Language

The site's own text describing the three subfamilies is precise and worth quoting in full:

- **Mono:** "Using the MONO style as the basis for the design, the constraints of fixed-width typesetting become guiding principles." Inktraps are present and visible here — ink pooling at corners — creating a subtle raw-print texture at display sizes. This is the most "typewriter" of the three.
- **Semi:** "The SEMI subfamily sits between the more mechanical MONO and the more actively rhythmical POLY designs." This is the transitional register — where the fixed-width logic begins to dissolve but has not yet become geometric.
- **Poly:** "The POLY subfamily is a geometric sans, contrasting wide circular shapes with narrow characters to create an active texture." Inktraps vanish entirely. The result is architecturally severe, highly legible at small sizes.

The Contrast section notes: "MONO and SEMI add texture through increased contrast, POLY has minimal contrast and retains a more geometric and constructed feel." The Inktraps section states they "are present in the MONO styles but vanish as the design progresses to the POLY tone" — treating the TONE axis as a continuous dissolution from mechanical to pure geometry.

### Layout and Structure

The page reads as a strictly vertical scroll with named sections functioning like chapters. There is minimal conventional navigation — only "Try" and "Buy" as action links alongside the type tester at the top. The top strip carries the numeric section identifiers (87.8, 91.2, etc.) as the primary navigation affordance. This is an anti-hierarchical structure: you are meant to read down, not jump around.

The type tester appears prominently near the top — a central, editable specimen area — before the deeper explanatory sections begin. The editorial copy (broadcast fiction) frames the specimens rather than being separated from them. Sections are divided by clear breaks rather than decorative rules, letting white space do the structural work.

The footer is minimal and typographically consistent with the rest of the page: **GRILLI-TYPE-INC. / BROOKLYN-NY-11211 / LUCERNE-CH-6006 / COPYRIGHT-2026** — set in all-caps, no logotype, no decorative treatment.

### Overall Tone and Feeling

The site communicates intellectual seriousness without solemnity. The broadcast fiction gives it warmth and wit; the typographic rigour gives it credibility. It feels like a system that has decided to introduce itself — measured, confident, slightly strange. The phrase "We are broadcasting into a shared field, where every presence adds structure, every response adds meaning" is both a description of how typography works in culture and a quietly beautiful piece of writing. The site never explains this double meaning; it trusts the reader to hear it.

The emotional register is: precision as a form of hospitality. The machine is trying to make contact. The typeface is the signal.

### What This Reference Contributes to WHATS

- **Broadcast/signal as card metaphor:** Each WHAT IS / WHAT IF card is a transmission into a shared field — not a polished document but a signal sent into a conversation. The "Confirm? Yes" prompt resonates directly with the question-and-response structure of the tool.
- **TONE axis thinking:** The idea of a continuous axis between mechanical-monospaced (Mono) and geometric-rhythmic (Poly) is a useful model for card register — some cards might feel more "Mono" (spare, clinical, fixed) and others more "Poly" (active, varied, constructed).
- **Copy voice:** The site's deadpan, systems-flavoured editorial voice — dry, specific, occasionally funny — is exactly the tone WHATS cards should aim for.
- **Question marks at display size:** The oversized punctuation of Mono, especially the question mark, is a direct visual argument for using the "?" prominently in WHAT IF? card headers. The punctuation carries emotional weight.

## Reference 2 — RE:CP (Cedric Price)

**Source:** https://www.metalocus.es/en/news/recp-cedric-price
**What it is:** *Re:CP* is a book by architect Cedric Price (1934–2003), edited by Hans-Ulrich Obrist, published by Birkhäuser Architecture (208 pp, paperback). Contributors include Arata Isozaki, Patrick Keiller, and Rem Koolhaas. The book collects writings and drawings across 40 years of practice and includes "The Invisible Sandwich," "Price Cuts" (illustrated collage study), and "73 Snacks" (73 vignettes on architecture). Over 150 colour and B&W illustrations.

### Cedric Price's Visual Language

Price was a radical British architect whose buildings were never meant to be permanent — architecture as process, not monument. His drawing language reflects this directly:

- **Hand-drawn on diazotype prints:** Marks in ink, crayon, pen, and graphite on blue-print stock. The ground is the technical drawing; the annotation is casual, human, urgent.
- **Grid-based organisation:** A recurring two-dimensional grid (4 columns × 3 rows) structures base drawings. Within the grid, cells are filled with small diagrams, cross-sections, and perspective vignettes — each self-contained, each part of a system.
- **Variable line weight as code:** Thick lines denote structure and load; thin lines denote possibility and movement. Line weight is a working notation, not an aesthetic choice.
- **Typewritten annotation:** Labels and notes are typewriter-set, flush-left, no hierarchy beyond case and proximity. The look of teletype output: monospaced, provisional, correctable.
- **Collage and ideogram:** Price mixed photographic cutouts, hand-lettered slogans, and diagrammatic icons within the same frame — a visual language closer to a working notebook than a presentation drawing.
- **Tone:** Economical, optimistic, sometimes funny. Images of people using spaces are important — users are shown as active agents, not background figures.

## How These Inform the WHATS Card Tool

### Typography

- **Primary display type:** GT Mechanik Mono or Semi at medium-to-heavy weights for card titles and category labels. The monospaced rhythm reinforces the existing system-print aesthetic and the oversized punctuation adds liveliness at display sizes.
- **Body / annotation type:** GT Mechanik Poly (Light or Regular) for card body text — more readable at small sizes while staying within the same typeface family. Alternatively, any clean monospace system font (Courier, Courier New) as a fallback to honour the typewriter-annotation register of Price's drawings.
- **Avoid:** Anything with high contrast stroke modulation (serifs, humanist sans). The current aesthetic and both references favour even-weight, engineered letterforms.

### Layout and Grid

- **Explicit grid lines:** Price's drawings make the underlying grid visible. Consider rendering faint grid lines or dot-grid backgrounds on cards — this aligns with the halftone dots already in the system and makes structure legible as a design element.
- **Card as cell:** Each WHAT IS / WHAT IF card should feel like one cell in a larger diagram — self-contained with a clear internal grid (label zone, body zone, tag zone), but connectable to adjacent cards.
- **Fixed-width thinking:** GT Mechanik's monospace logic — consistent horizontal slots — translates to CSS as fixed-width card containers with consistent internal padding. Cards should never reflow unpredictably; their geometry is part of the information.

### Colour and Texture

- **Maintain the limited palette:** Both references favour restraint. Price's drawings use blue-print paper, black ink, and occasional red or yellow highlight. GT Mechanik's specimen is high-contrast, largely black on white.
- **Paper tone backgrounds:** Consider a warm off-white or blueprint-blue ground for cards rather than pure white — this connects to diazotype paper and feels less screen-native.
- **Ink texture at large sizes:** If halftone dots are used, apply them more heavily in solid fills rather than backgrounds, echoing the pooled-ink character of inktraps.

### Graphic Language

- **Annotation marks:** Adopt Price's notation vocabulary — bracket annotations, dashed lines indicating possibility/movement, ruled underlines for headings — as decorative-functional CSS border patterns or SVG marks on cards.
- **Typewritten provisionality:** Cards should feel like working documents, not polished deliverables. Slightly irregular visual rhythm (achieved through monospaced type) reinforces this.
- **Oversized punctuation as accent:** Use colons, dashes, and question marks (integral to WHAT IS? / WHAT IF?) at visually prominent sizes in card headers — directly exploiting GT Mechanik's oversized-punctuation character.

## Combined Design Direction

The next visual design pass should feel like: **a Cedric Price working notebook that has been typeset by a Swiss systems designer on a 1970s teleprinter.**

Specific qualities to aim for:
- Grid is visible and structural, not decorative
- Type is monospaced, even-weight, slightly naïve — confident rather than polished
- Colour is rationed (2–3 colours maximum per view)
- Marks and lines have working-drawing energy: dashes, brackets, variable weight
- Cards feel provisional and connectable — cells in a flexible system, not finished objects
- Question marks are big and proud

This direction sharpens the existing Mac System 6 / Risograph aesthetic by adding intellectual seriousness (Swiss grid discipline, Price's anti-monument ethos) while keeping the playful, lo-fi print quality already established.
